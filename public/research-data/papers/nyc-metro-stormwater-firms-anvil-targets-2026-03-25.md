## Executive Summary

The NYC tri-state stormwater engineering market is in a historic spending cycle — NYC DEP alone has committed **$3.5 billion** to green infrastructure, on top of a broader **$20 billion** climate resilience strategy. New Jersey just reclassified all municipalities to Tier A MS4 status, creating an overnight surge in compliance demand. Connecticut's MS4 general permit requires stormwater management plans from every covered municipality. This regulatory tsunami is creating more work than the Arcadis/Jacobs/Hazen tier can absorb, opening massive opportunity for small and mid-tier firms.

The research identified **15-20 regional firms** with 5-100 employees that are deeply embedded in tri-state stormwater work but are almost certainly running on AutoCAD, Excel, and email-based workflows. These firms represent the ideal Project Anvil early adopter: hungry for competitive edge, too small to build internal tools, and operating in a market where the ability to produce and iterate on PFDs collaboratively could directly win them larger contracts.

**The top 5 target firms for outreach, ranked by fit:**

## Market Context: Why Now

### The Money
- **NYC DEP** committed **$1.5B** for green infrastructure in combined sewer areas, plus **$2B more** for separately sewered areas
- **$6.2B** in grey infrastructure projects currently in the pipeline
- Since 2010, NYC has invested **$1B** in green infrastructure and installed **13,000+ GI assets** (rain gardens, bioswales, permeable pavement, detention basins)
- In 2024 alone, NYC built **~1,500 curbside rain gardens and infiltration basins**
- NYS Governor Hochul: **$60M** in green infrastructure grants, **$269M** for local water infrastructure, part of the **$4.2B Environmental Bond Act**
- For every dollar NYC spends on blue-green infrastructure, it gets **$2.09** back in socio-economic benefits

### The Regulatory Pressure
**New Jersey** (most aggressive):
- All NJ municipalities reclassified to **Tier A MS4** as of Jan 1, 2023 — previously 100+ were Tier B with lighter requirements
- Municipalities must complete **comprehensive MS4 stormwater infrastructure maps** by 2026
- NJ PACT REAL land use reforms (published Aug 2024) will further tighten stormwater design standards
- Inland Flood Protection Rule (effective July 2023) requires BMPs designed for **future storm conditions**, not just current

**Connecticut:**
- MS4 General Permit requires **Stormwater Management Plans** from all permittees
- 6 minimum control measures required
- Annual reports due to CT DEEP by April 1

**New York:**
- NYSDEC SPDES Construction General Permit requires SWPPPs
- NYC Unified Stormwater Rule compliance
- Combined Sewer Overflow (CSO) Long Term Control Plans driving massive design work

## Competitive Dynamics: How Small Firms Win

The research surfaced clear patterns for how small stormwater firms compete against the big players:

1. **Local relationships trump scale.** Small firms win municipal contracts because they know the local DPW director, attend the planning board meetings, and answer the phone on Saturday. Arcadis can't do that for a 10,000-person NJ township.

2. **Specialization beats breadth.** A firm like Princeton Hydro that lives and breathes nature-based stormwater can out-design a generalist Jacobs team on green infrastructure projects.

3. **MBE/WBE/DBE set-asides.** NYC DEP and NJ agencies have small business and diversity set-aside requirements for subcontracting. Small firms get pulled into large projects as required subs, then build relationships for direct awards.

4. **Speed and cost.** Small firms can turn around a SWPPP or stormwater management plan in weeks, not months. Municipal clients with tight budgets prefer this.

5. **The gap:** Where small firms consistently lose is on **technology and presentation quality**. The big firms can produce polished, coordinated deliverables using enterprise tools. A collaborative PFD editor could close this gap.

## Technology Posture: The Opportunity

Based on the ENR data and general AEC industry research:
- Top 500 design firm revenue jumped **33.5%** since pre-pandemic 2020
- More firms are integrating AI and digital tools into operations (2024-2025 trend)
- High-growth firms are **2x more likely** to adopt digital/content marketing approaches
- But small firms (sub-50 employees) typically run on:
  - **AutoCAD** or Civil3D for design
  - **HydroCAD** or **StormCAD** for stormwater modeling
  - **Excel** for calculations and tracking
  - **Email + PDF markups** for collaboration
  - **No collaborative design tools** — PFDs are drawn in AutoCAD or Visio, exported as PDFs, redlined by hand or in Bluebeam

**The pain point:** When a small firm needs to iterate on a process flow diagram with a municipal client or subconsultant, they're emailing PDFs back and forth. Version control is a folder named "FINAL_v3_REVISED_ACTUAL." A tool like Anvil that enables real-time collaborative PFD editing, version-controlled through git, would be a genuine competitive differentiator.

## Recommended Outreach Strategy

1. **Lead with the NJ opportunity.** The MS4 Tier A reclassification and 2026 infrastructure mapping deadline are creating immediate, urgent demand. NJ-based firms are the hungriest right now.

2. **Target firms doing green infrastructure design.** GI projects (bioswales, rain gardens, detention systems) require iterative design with multiple stakeholders (client, regulator, landscape architect, civil engineer). This is where collaborative PFDs add the most value.

3. **Position Anvil as a "compete with the big firms" tool, not an "efficiency" tool.** Small firm principals don't buy efficiency software — they buy tools that help them win bigger contracts. Frame it as: "Now your 15-person firm can produce deliverables that look like they came from Hazen."

4. **Start with 3 conversations:**
   - **eDesign Dynamics** — NYC, pure GI focus, small team
   - **Princeton Hydro** — NJ, innovation-minded, nature-based stormwater
   - **Careaga Engineering** — NJ, small, hyper-local, 30-year relationships

5. **Attend NJWEA (NJ Water Environment Association) or ACEC-NJ events** to meet these firms in person. The stormwater engineering community in the tri-state area is small and relationship-driven.
