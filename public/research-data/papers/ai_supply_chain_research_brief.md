# AI Supply Chain Profit Capture: 1-3 Year Research Brief

**Date:** March 26, 2026  
**Prepared for:** User  
**Classification:** General Research

## Executive Summary

The AI infrastructure buildout is still in its capex-heavy phase, and over the next 1-3 years the largest share of incremental profit is still likely to accrue to the most constrained, differentiated upstream suppliers rather than to the broadest set of downstream operators. The clearest near-term winners are `NVIDIA`, `TSMC`, `Broadcom`, and `SK hynix`, with `Micron` moving into the first tier as HBM and data-center DRAM tighten. These companies sit at the most valuable control points in the stack: accelerator architecture, advanced networking, high-bandwidth memory, and leading-edge manufacturing plus advanced packaging.

Hyperscalers are simultaneously the biggest spenders and among the biggest absolute profit pools, but their AI economics are mixed. `Microsoft`, `Amazon/AWS`, and `Alphabet/Google Cloud` are likely to capture very large absolute dollars of profit because they own customer relationships and can cross-sell AI into cloud, productivity, ads, and developer platforms. However, their **incremental AI profit margins** are lower and less visible in the next 12-24 months than those of the leading chip suppliers, because they are carrying depreciation, power, leasing, and model-serving costs ahead of full utilization and pricing maturity. `Meta` is a major beneficiary in engagement and ad yield, but near-term value capture is more indirect and less cleanly monetized than at cloud peers.

Server OEMs, power/cooling vendors, and data-center developers should all see strong revenue growth, but their profit capture will be more uneven. `Dell`, `HPE`, and `Supermicro` benefit from AI server volume, yet the assembly layer remains structurally lower margin and more competitive. `Vertiv`, `Eaton`, `Schneider Electric`, and `Trane` are well positioned because AI clusters raise power density and liquid-cooling requirements; these businesses can earn solid incremental margins, but the total profit pool is still smaller than semiconductors and hyperscale cloud. `Equinix` and `Digital Realty` should continue to monetize scarcity in powered, connected capacity, while `CoreWeave` shows how large AI revenue can coexist with weak net profitability when financing costs and capacity timing dominate the P&L.

Base case: over the next 1-3 years, the AI supply chain remains **profit-first upstream, revenue-first downstream**. If the capex cycle persists and utilization stays high, the top profit capturers should be:

1. `NVIDIA`
2. `Microsoft`, `Amazon/AWS`, and `Alphabet/Google Cloud` in absolute profit dollars
3. `TSMC`
4. `Broadcom`
5. `SK hynix`
6. `Micron`
7. `Vertiv` and `Eaton` among infrastructure enablers
8. `Equinix` and `Digital Realty` among data-center landlords/operators
9. `Dell`, `HPE`, and `Supermicro`

The main caveat is that this ranking changes if inference economics deteriorate sharply, export controls tighten further, or model efficiency reduces demand for top-end training clusters faster than expected.

## Assumptions and Method

Because most companies do not report "AI-only" profit, this brief uses the following assumptions:

- **Time horizon:** March 2026 through March 2029.
- **Metric:** estimated ability to capture **incremental AI-linked operating profit**, with separate notes where total corporate profit is more relevant than AI-only profit.
- **Base case:** hyperscaler capex plans broadly hold, GPU/HBM supply remains tight but improving, and no severe macro recession disrupts enterprise/cloud demand.
- **Attribution rule:** when a company discloses total segment profit but not AI-specific profit, AI-linked profit is inferred from product mix, backlog, management commentary, and the share of growth clearly tied to AI infrastructure.
- **Confidence:** highest for companies with explicit AI revenue disclosures (`NVIDIA`, `Broadcom`, `Micron`, `CoreWeave`); lower for diversified firms where AI is embedded in broader segments (`Microsoft`, `Amazon`, `Alphabet`, `Eaton`, `Schneider`, `Trane`).

## Current Landscape

### Why the profit pool is upstream

AI infrastructure remains constrained at four choke points:

- leading accelerators
- high-bandwidth memory
- advanced foundry capacity
- advanced packaging and high-speed networking

When demand exceeds supply at choke points, pricing power and gross margins concentrate there. That is why `NVIDIA` reported fiscal 2026 revenue of `$215.9 billion`, data-center revenue of `$193.7 billion`, and fiscal 2026 non-GAAP gross margin of `71.3%`. Those are exceptional economics for a hardware supplier and far above the economics of server assembly or colocation.

The same dynamic is visible one layer down. `Broadcom` said fiscal Q1 2026 AI revenue reached `$8.4 billion`, up `106%` year over year, and it guided to `$10.7 billion` of AI semiconductor revenue in Q2. `Micron` reported fiscal Q1 2026 gross margin of `56.8%`, with its cloud memory business posting `66%` gross margin and `55%` operating margin. `SK hynix` reported FY2025 operating profit of `47.2 trillion won` on `97.1 trillion won` of revenue, a reminder that HBM has transformed memory from a cyclical commodity story into a constrained, premium product story.

### Why hyperscalers still matter

The demand side is increasingly dominated by a small number of buyers. Public disclosures from `Alphabet`, `Amazon`, and `Microsoft` point to an unprecedented 2026 capex cycle, while `Meta` explicitly guided `2026 capital expenditures of $115-$135 billion` after reporting `2025 capex of $72.22 billion`.

These companies matter because they decide:

- how fast clusters are deployed
- whether demand tilts toward GPUs or custom ASICs
- whether owned data centers or leased capacity win
- how much value remains with infrastructure providers versus application/platform layers

### Core concepts

#### Absolute profit pool vs. incremental AI profit pool

`AWS`, `Microsoft`, and `Alphabet` may capture more total profit dollars than many suppliers because their businesses are much larger. But the **incremental profit created by the AI buildout** is currently concentrated further upstream, where component scarcity is strongest.

#### Training vs. inference

Training drove the first wave of spending. Inference now matters more for sustainable value capture because it recurs with user demand. Suppliers that lower cost per token without surrendering pricing power will capture more durable profits. That supports `NVIDIA`, `Broadcom` networking, and memory vendors, but it also favors hyperscalers if they can keep inference utilization high and price AI services rationally.

#### AI server economics

An AI server is not a normal server. The highest-value components are GPUs/XPUs, HBM, networking, and increasingly power and liquid-cooling systems. OEMs assemble and integrate these systems, but the gross-margin stack is dominated by the silicon and memory layers.

#### Power density as a new bottleneck

AI racks consume far more power than traditional enterprise compute. That raises the value of switchgear, thermal systems, liquid cooling, busways, backup power, and grid access. This is why equipment suppliers and data-center developers have become credible second-order AI winners.

## Key Players and Positioning

### 1. Hyperscalers

#### Microsoft

`Microsoft` remains one of the strongest candidates for medium-term AI value capture because it monetizes across Azure, Microsoft 365 Copilot, GitHub, and enterprise software. In FY26 Q2, Azure and other cloud services revenue grew `39%`, and Microsoft disclosed `Additions to property and equipment` of `$49.3 billion` for the first six months of FY26, versus `$30.7 billion` a year earlier. That spending is pressuring cloud margin, but it is also building a privileged distribution position.

View: `Microsoft` is likely a top-three absolute profit capturer by 2027-2028, but near-term AI-specific margin capture is less visible than at `NVIDIA`.

#### Amazon / AWS

`AWS` remains the cleanest hyperscaler infrastructure monetization story because customers buy raw and managed AI capacity directly. Amazon reported FY2025 AWS revenue of `$128.7 billion` and operating income of `$45.6 billion`; Q4 2025 AWS operating income was `$12.5 billion`. Public reporting on the earnings call indicates Amazon expects about `$200 billion` of 2026 capex, mostly for AWS and AI infrastructure.

View: `AWS` should capture massive absolute profit, but free-cash-flow conversion may stay under pressure while capex runs far ahead of depreciation recovery.

#### Alphabet / Google Cloud

Alphabet is showing the strongest visible AI-related acceleration among cloud peers. In Q4 2025, Google Cloud revenue rose `48%` to `$17.7 billion`, Cloud operating income more than doubled to `$5.3 billion`, and cloud backlog reached `$240 billion`. Alphabet also guided `2026 capex of $175-$185 billion`.

View: `Alphabet` is moving from "AI spender" to "AI monetizer." If current cloud margin expansion continues, it could become one of the biggest positive revisions in AI profit capture over the next 24 months.

#### Meta

Meta is spending at hyperscaler scale, but its near-term monetization is indirect: better recommendations, ad targeting, engagement, and internal productivity rather than primarily selling compute. That can still be lucrative, but it is harder to isolate and more sensitive to ad-market conditions.

View: strong strategic position, but weaker near-term direct AI profit capture than `Microsoft`, `AWS`, or `Google Cloud`.

### 2. GPU and Networking Vendors

#### NVIDIA

This remains the dominant profit pool in the stack. Fiscal 2026 net income was roughly `$117.0 billion`, with Q4 fiscal 2026 data-center revenue at `$62.3 billion` and gross margin above `75%` in the quarter. NVIDIA also benefits from owning not just GPUs but increasingly the full accelerated stack: networking, interconnect, software, reference systems, and roadmaps that customers design around years in advance.

View: still the most advantaged player for the next 1-3 years. The main risk is not demand collapse; it is whether competition or customer insourcing compresses pricing faster than expected.

#### Broadcom

Broadcom is the clearest alternative profit winner, especially if custom accelerators continue to scale at hyperscalers. With Q1 FY2026 AI revenue of `$8.4 billion` and Q2 guidance of `$10.7 billion`, Broadcom is no longer a peripheral AI beneficiary. It is central to both custom XPU programs and AI networking.

View: likely top-five in AI-linked operating profit by 2027. It benefits if hyperscalers diversify away from a single accelerator supplier.

### 3. Memory

#### SK hynix

HBM leadership is translating directly into profit capture. FY2025 revenue was `97.1 trillion won`, operating profit `47.2 trillion won`, and management highlighted HBM and AI server products as key drivers. This is one of the purest examples of a component company moving from cyclical margins to structurally elevated margins.

View: one of the biggest AI profit winners globally, especially if HBM4 ramps on time.

#### Micron

Micron is improving rapidly. Fiscal Q1 2026 revenue reached `$13.64 billion`, non-GAAP gross margin reached `56.8%`, and the cloud memory business posted `66%` gross margin. The company is now an essential AI memory supplier rather than a lagging participant.

View: likely a major earnings revision story over the next 12 months, but still behind `SK hynix` in current HBM leadership.

### 4. Foundries and Advanced Packaging

#### TSMC

TSMC is the key manufacturing bottleneck. Q4 2025 revenue was `$33.73 billion`, gross margin `62.3%`, and operating margin `54.0%`. Independent reporting tied to the earnings call indicates HPC was `58%` of 2025 revenue and AI accelerators were roughly `55%` of Q4 revenue. TSMC also signaled `2026 capex of roughly $52-$56 billion`, with `10-20%` allocated to packaging.

View: TSMC is the most secure foundry profit winner because AI demand is pulling not only wafers but also CoWoS and other advanced packaging. It may be less visible than `NVIDIA`, but it is one of the most defensible profit pools in the chain.

### 5. Server OEMs

#### Dell

Dell's AI server business is scaling fast. Publicly available FY2026 Q4 materials indicate roughly `$24.7 billion` of AI-optimized server revenue in FY26 and a backlog of about `$43 billion` entering FY27. That is large, but OEM economics remain lower margin than silicon.

View: major revenue beneficiary, moderate profit beneficiary.

#### HPE

HPE reported Q1 FY2026 revenue of `$9.3 billion`, with Cloud & AI revenue of `$6.3 billion` and improved profitability. The Juniper combination also strengthens HPE in AI networking. Still, margins remain much lower than those of the leading semiconductor suppliers.

View: credible share gainer in enterprise AI infrastructure, but not a top-tier profit capturer.

#### Supermicro

Supermicro remains highly exposed to AI server growth but structurally vulnerable to margin pressure and governance risk. In Q3 FY2025, gross margin fell to `9.6%` from `15.5%` a year earlier, despite strong AI positioning. Recent export-control-related legal issues further raise risk.

View: revenue leverage is high, but profit durability is weak.

### 6. Power and Cooling Providers

#### Vertiv

Vertiv is one of the strongest second-order AI winners. In Q4 2025 it reported net sales of `$2.88 billion`, adjusted operating margin of `23.2%`, organic orders growth of `252%`, backlog of `$15.0 billion`, and 2026 sales guidance of `$13.25-$13.75 billion`.

View: among non-silicon players, `Vertiv` is one of the best positioned to capture incremental profit.

#### Eaton

Eaton reported record Q4 2025 results and specifically cited data-center momentum in Electrical Americas, where twelve-month rolling average orders were up `16%`. This is not a pure-play AI name, but AI is becoming a meaningful growth and backlog driver.

View: steady, diversified beneficiary with durable pricing power in electrical infrastructure.

#### Schneider Electric and Trane

Schneider is well positioned in electrical architecture, distribution, and reference designs for AI data centers. Trane's Q4 2025 results showed strong commercial HVAC momentum, record backlog of `$7.8 billion`, and Americas Commercial HVAC bookings up more than `35%`.

View: attractive second-order beneficiaries, though the profit pool remains smaller than in semis and cloud.

### 7. Data-Center Developers and Operators

#### Equinix

Equinix reported Q4 2025 record annualized gross bookings of `$474 million`, up `42%` year over year, and highlighted sustained AI demand across infrastructure and interconnection. Equinix is especially well positioned where customers need proximity, neutral interconnection, and metro-adjacent capacity rather than isolated hyperscale campuses.

View: strong and durable, but AI-driven upside is steadier than explosive.

#### Digital Realty

Digital Realty reported Q4 2025 bookings expected to generate `$400 million` of annualized GAAP rental revenue at 100% share, with signed-but-not-commenced backlog of `$817 million` and 2026 Core FFO guidance of `$7.90-$8.00` per share.

View: attractive value capture from scarcity in powered land and signed backlog; more sensitive than Equinix to development timing and capital markets.

#### CoreWeave

CoreWeave shows the difference between demand and profit. It reached `$5.13 billion` of 2025 revenue and `$66.8 billion` of revenue backlog, with contracted power of about `3.1 GW`, but still posted an operating loss and a net loss due to the capital structure and interest burden.

View: major revenue growth story, not yet a major profit capture story.

## Recent Developments That Matter

- Hyperscaler capex guidance has moved higher, not lower, despite periodic "AI bubble" concerns.
- AI networking has become a meaningful profit pool in its own right, helping `NVIDIA` and `Broadcom`.
- HBM is no longer a peripheral component; it is a core strategic bottleneck shaping accelerator shipments and margins.
- Advanced packaging has become as important as leading-edge wafer capacity for near-term supply.
- Power availability, grid interconnection, and liquid cooling are now material gating factors for cluster deployment.
- Data-center leasing backlogs suggest demand is extending beyond immediate training clusters into broader AI and cloud rollouts.

## Who Is Positioned To Capture the Most Profit

### Ranked outlook: next 1-3 years

#### Tier 1: strongest expected profit capture

- `NVIDIA`
- `TSMC`
- `Broadcom`
- `SK hynix`

These firms combine scarce supply, strong pricing power, and visible order pull. Their margins are already high, and incremental volume tends to fall through efficiently.

#### Tier 2: very large absolute profit capture, but with monetization lag

- `Microsoft`
- `Amazon/AWS`
- `Alphabet/Google Cloud`
- `Micron`

The cloud providers will almost certainly generate very large AI-linked gross profit dollars, but they are also bearing the heaviest depreciation and infrastructure cost. `Micron` belongs here because its margin profile is improving rapidly, but its starting point is lower than `SK hynix`.

#### Tier 3: strong enablers, smaller pool

- `Vertiv`
- `Eaton`
- `Equinix`
- `Digital Realty`

These players benefit from the physical reality of AI deployments. The profit pool is meaningful and should compound, but it is smaller than semis and hyperscale software/cloud.

#### Tier 4: revenue winners, modest profit capture

- `Dell`
- `HPE`
- `Supermicro`
- `CoreWeave`

This group can grow fast, but margins are constrained by competition, financing, or commodity-like integration economics.

## Risks, Challenges, and Open Questions

### Risks

- **Inference pricing compression:** if token prices fall faster than costs, hyperscaler ROI could disappoint.
- **Model efficiency improvements:** better algorithms or hardware utilization could reduce demand for the most expensive compute.
- **Export controls:** tighter U.S. restrictions could hit `NVIDIA`, memory vendors, and server OEMs.
- **Packaging and HBM bottlenecks:** shortages could delay system shipments even if GPU demand remains strong.
- **Power and permitting constraints:** data-center projects can slip despite customer demand.
- **Capital markets risk:** developers and AI clouds with heavy debt loads are vulnerable if financing costs stay elevated.

### Open questions

- Will hyperscalers favor more `NVIDIA` GPUs or accelerate custom ASIC deployment?
- Can `Broadcom` and custom silicon programs materially erode `NVIDIA`'s economic moat by 2028?
- Does inference become a hyperscaler margin engine or a utility-like low-margin service?
- How much of the current buildout is true utilization demand versus strategic capacity preemption?
- Will enterprises buy AI through public cloud, on-prem systems, or colocated private infrastructure?

## Practical Opportunities

- Favor companies with **structural scarcity** rather than simple unit exposure.
- Watch for vendors that convert AI demand into **backlog plus margin expansion**, not just backlog.
- In downstream layers, prefer businesses with pricing power tied to power, cooling, land, or interconnection scarcity.
- Distinguish between **revenue growth** and **economic value capture**; `CoreWeave` is the clearest example of the difference.
- For corporate strategy, the best near-term opportunities are in advanced packaging, liquid cooling, electrical distribution, AI networking, and power-constrained data-center development.

## What to Monitor in the Next 12 Months

- `NVIDIA` data-center gross margin and the pace of Blackwell/Rubin mix shift
- `Broadcom` AI semiconductor revenue trajectory beyond Q2 FY2026
- HBM4 qualification and shipment timing at `SK hynix`, `Micron`, and `Samsung`
- `TSMC` CoWoS and advanced packaging capacity expansion
- actual 2026 capex execution at `Microsoft`, `Amazon`, `Alphabet`, and `Meta`
- AI cloud revenue mix and margin disclosures from hyperscalers
- AI server backlog conversion at `Dell` and `HPE`
- `Vertiv` order growth, book-to-bill, and backlog conversion
- power-availability announcements in Northern Virginia, Texas, Phoenix, and major international hubs
- signed-but-not-commenced backlog conversion at `Digital Realty` and xScale/AI leasing momentum at `Equinix`
- whether `CoreWeave` can improve operating leverage faster than interest expense grows
- any new export-control actions affecting China-bound accelerators, networking gear, or AI servers

## Actionable Next Steps

1. Separate your analysis into **incremental AI-linked profit** and **total corporate profit** before comparing winners; otherwise hyperscalers and component suppliers get mixed together incorrectly.
2. Build a quarterly tracker for `NVIDIA`, `Broadcom`, `TSMC`, `SK hynix`, `Micron`, `Microsoft`, `AWS`, and `Alphabet`, focused on backlog, capex, gross margin, and AI-specific revenue disclosures.
3. Treat `Vertiv`, `Eaton`, `Equinix`, and `Digital Realty` as the highest-quality second-order beneficiaries and monitor whether their backlogs convert without project delays.
4. Be cautious on server OEMs unless evidence emerges that AI mix is improving margins rather than only driving volume.
5. Use power availability and packaging capacity as leading indicators; they are now as important as end-demand commentary.
6. Re-underwrite the thesis after each hyperscaler earnings cycle, because the biggest near-term swing factor is still whether capex plans convert into durable, monetized utilization.

## Bottom Line

For the next 1-3 years, the AI supply chain should remain a story of **concentrated profit capture upstream and broader but thinner revenue capture downstream**. `NVIDIA` is still the strongest individual profit winner. `TSMC`, `Broadcom`, `SK hynix`, and increasingly `Micron` are the next most advantaged. `Microsoft`, `AWS`, and `Google Cloud` should capture enormous absolute profit, but the market still has to see cleaner incremental AI margins. Power/cooling providers and data-center developers are credible second-order winners. Server OEMs and highly levered AI clouds are likely to capture growth, but not the largest share of economic rents.
