## The Nigerian Traditional Ceremony — What You Need to Know

### Key Components (Varies by Ethnicity)

**Yoruba Traditional Wedding (Igbeyawo):**
- **Introduction (Mo mi i mo e):** Families formally meet. This may happen months before the wedding day itself or be combined into the wedding day.
- **Engagement list (Eru Iyawo):** The bride's family provides a list of items the groom's family must bring — this can include fabric, shoes, bags, food items, drinks, a Bible or Quran, honey, sugar, salt, kola nuts, bitter kola, palm oil, and more. The list is highly specific and varies by family.
- **Alaga Iduro & Alaga Ijoko:** Two MCs — one for the groom's side (Iduro, who stands) and one for the bride's side (Ijoko, who sits). They run the entire ceremony with humor, songs, and tradition. Hiring great alagas is one of the most important decisions.
- **Aso-oke:** The traditional woven fabric for bride and groom. You'll choose colors, style, and a designer/tailor. The bride typically has at least 2 outfit changes.
- **Prostrating (Dobale):** The groom and his friends prostrate before the bride's family. This is a significant cultural moment.
- **Letter reading and bride price:** Symbolic negotiation, usually lighthearted.
- **Bride's entrance:** Multiple dramatic entrances, often with outfit changes and dancing.
- **Feeding ceremony:** Bride and groom feed each other honey, signifying sweetness in marriage.

**Igbo Traditional Wedding (Igba Nkwu / Wine Carrying):**
- **Iku Aka (Knocking on the door):** The groom's family formally visits the bride's family to declare intent.
- **Bride price negotiation:** More structured than Yoruba — involves specific lists and negotiations.
- **Wine carrying:** The bride carries a cup of palm wine through the crowd to find her groom and present it to him. This is the ceremony's climax.
- **Traditional attire:** Isiagu (embroidered top) for men, George wrapper and blouse for women, coral beads.

### Decisions Unique to the Traditional Ceremony
1. **How traditional vs. modern?** Some couples do a full multi-hour traditional ceremony; others do a condensed modern version. Discuss with your parents early.
2. **Who controls the engagement list?** Traditionally, the bride's family sets it. Some families go extravagant, others keep it reasonable. This is a key negotiation point.
3. **Aso-ebi:** This is a *big* logistical and financial decision. You choose fabric, set a price per yard, and guests/family buy it to wear to your wedding as a show of solidarity. Decisions include: how many color variants, price point (this can be contentious), who is expected to buy it, and whether you subsidize it for your wedding party.
4. **Live band vs. DJ:** Many Nigerian traditional weddings feature a live juju or highlife band. This is a different vibe from a DJ and is often considered more appropriate for the traditional setting.
5. **Food:** Nigerian wedding food is its own universe — jollof rice (the centerpiece), pounded yam, egusi, suya, small chops (spring rolls, samosa, puff puff, chicken), and more. You'll need to decide on a caterer who specializes in Nigerian cuisine.
6. **Money spraying:** During dancing, guests "spray" (place money on) the couple. Decide how you feel about this, whether to have someone collecting, and how to manage it.

## Managing the "Two Weddings" Dynamic

### Making Each Day Distinct
The biggest risk with back-to-back ceremonies is that one feels like a repeat or afterthought. Design each day with its own identity:
- **Saturday (Traditional):** Rich in culture, color, family heritage, Nigerian music, Nigerian food. This is where your roots are celebrated.
- **Sunday (Western):** Classic elegance, your personal love story, vows, first dance, wedding cake. This is where your partnership takes center stage.

### Guest List Strategy
- **Option A: Same guest list for both.** Simplest, but means a big weekend commitment for guests.
- **Option B: Traditional is a smaller, mostly-family affair; Western is the bigger celebration.** Common and practical.
- **Option C: Traditional is the bigger Nigerian celebration; Western is more intimate.** Less common but valid.
- Decide early — this drives budget, venue capacity, and invitations.

### Vendor Overlap
Some vendors can do double duty (photographer, videographer, planner), which may save money via package deals. Others will necessarily be different (caterer, music, possibly decor). Negotiate weekend packages where possible.

## Budget Framework

### How to Think About Splitting Costs

A rough framework for a dual-ceremony wedding budget:

| Category | % of Total Budget |
|----------|------------------|
| Venues (already booked) | 25-30% |
| Catering (both days) | 25-30% |
| Photography/Videography | 8-12% |
| Attire (all outfits, both days) | 8-10% |
| Music/Entertainment (both days) | 5-8% |
| Decor/Florals (both days) | 5-10% |
| Stationery & Printing | 2-3% |
| Beauty (hair, makeup) | 2-3% |
| Transportation & Logistics | 2-3% |
| Favors, gifts, tips | 2-3% |
| Contingency buffer | 5-10% |

### Managing Parental Contributions
- **Have the money conversation early and explicitly.** Ask each set of parents: "What is the total amount you'd like to contribute?" and "Are there specific things you'd like your contribution to go toward?"
- **Clarify whether contributions come with decision-making power.** In Nigerian families especially, financial contribution often implies input on decisions. If a parent is paying for the traditional ceremony, they may expect significant say over the guest list, food, and decor.
- **Track everything in one shared budget spreadsheet/tool.** Having two ceremonies with multiple funding sources gets confusing fast. Use a tool like Google Sheets, Airtable, or a wedding budget app.
- **Build in a buffer.** Dual ceremonies have more surprise costs. Budget 5-10% contingency.

### Hidden Costs to Watch For
- **Aso-ebi coordination:** Even if guests buy their own, you may end up subsidizing for the wedding party
- **Tips for vendors** (often forgotten — budget 15-20% of vendor costs)
- **Alterations** for all outfits across both days
- **Welcome bags** and hospitality costs for out-of-town guests staying the whole weekend
- **Post-wedding brunch** (if hosting)
- **Marriage license fees, officiant fees**
- **Overtime fees** if either event runs long
- **Delivery and setup fees** from vendors (often separate from service fees)
- **Second-day venue flip** — if using different decor, someone has to set up Sunday after Saturday's event

## Things Couples Often Overlook or Wish They'd Done Earlier

1. **Feeding yourselves.** Brides and grooms famously don't eat at their own weddings. With TWO days of events, plan for this — have someone bring you plates, or schedule a private meal moment.

2. **Energy management across the weekend.** Saturday traditional + Sunday western is a marathon. Build in rest. Consider a late-morning Sunday start to recover from Saturday.

3. **The "in-between" logistics.** What happens Saturday night after the traditional? Sunday morning before the wedding? Plan these transitions — they're where stress hides.

4. **Vendor meals.** You need to feed your photographer, videographer, DJ, and planner at both events. This is often a contractual requirement and adds to catering costs.

5. **A dedicated person collecting monetary gifts.** Nigerian weddings involve significant cash gifts and money spraying. Designate trusted people to collect and secure it.

6. **Aso-ebi drama.** Fabric selection, pricing, and distribution causes more wedding-related arguments than almost anything else. Start early, set clear expectations, and don't take it personally if some people don't buy it.

7. **Two sets of decor.** If your venues need separate decoration for Saturday and Sunday, someone needs to coordinate the Saturday teardown and Sunday setup. This is often where a planner earns their fee.

8. **Guest communication.** For a dual-ceremony weekend, guests need more information than a typical wedding — which events they're invited to, dress code for each day (traditional attire Saturday, formal Sunday), logistics, parking, hotels. Your wedding website is critical.

9. **Dress code clarity.** Be explicit: "Saturday: traditional attire / aso-ebi. Sunday: formal/cocktail." Guests unfamiliar with Nigerian weddings will especially appreciate guidance.

10. **The marriage license timing.** You can only legally marry once — decide which ceremony includes the legal signing (usually Sunday), and make sure the license is obtained with the right timing for your state.
