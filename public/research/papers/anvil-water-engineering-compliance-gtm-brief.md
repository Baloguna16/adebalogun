# Anvil in Water Engineering Compliance: GTM and Wedge Analysis

**Date:** March 26, 2026  
**Prepared for:** Anvil  
**Classification:** General Research

## Executive Summary

**Bottom line:** the fastest path to a beachhead for Anvil is not to sell "document automation for all water compliance." It is to win a narrow, painful, document-centric workflow where teams already feel the administrative burden, where the underlying forms are standardized enough to templatize, and where the buyer can purchase without a long public-sector procurement cycle.

Under those constraints, the best initial wedge is **stormwater construction compliance**, specifically the **SWPPP/NOI/inspection/enforcement document package** used by regional civil and environmental engineering firms and stormwater service providers. This wedge outperforms drinking-water reporting, wastewater permitting, and utility-wide compliance programs on speed-to-value because it combines recurring projects, high document volume, multi-party signatures, jurisdiction-specific templates, and acute schedule sensitivity. A missed or delayed filing can stall site work; that makes the ROI legible and urgent.

The recommended initial buyer is a **stormwater or water resources practice leader, compliance operations leader, or principal at a 20-200 employee regional engineering firm** with a dedicated stormwater team. These firms are large enough to have repeated workflow pain and enough project volume to justify software, but small enough to avoid the slowest enterprise procurement motions. They also have a direct economic incentive: every hour spent assembling compliance packets is high-cost engineer or project-manager time that could otherwise be billed to clients or redeployed to more technical work.

The best initial geography is **California first, Texas second, and a small set of fast-follow Sun Belt states after that**. California is the strongest initial beachhead because the state has a mature digital filing environment, explicit SWPPP-related professional roles such as QSD/QSP under the 2022 Construction General Permit, and a high-complexity compliance culture. Texas is attractive next because of sustained construction volume and broad use of construction stormwater permits, but California is the better design partner market because workflow rigor is higher and the pain is easier to quantify.

Pricing should be **hybrid usage plus platform**, not pure seat-based SaaS. The recommended model is a base annual platform fee per firm or business unit, plus usage bands tied to active projects, completed permit packets, or submitted compliance packages. The pilot should be a **90-day, 10-25 project implementation** focused on a small number of high-value templates and one state first. The ROI story should not rely on vague "automation" language. It should quantify engineer hours saved, faster first-submittal turnaround, fewer resubmissions, fewer schedule delays, and better recovery of billable capacity.

The strategic caution is that this market already has vertical incumbents in stormwater and water utility compliance, including OpenGov, SwiftComply/NPDESPro/CloudCompli, 2NDNATURE, and 120Water. Anvil should therefore **avoid positioning as a full compliance operating system** in the first phase. The winning posture is: **the fastest and most configurable document infrastructure layer for compliance-heavy engineering work**, with the option to expand upstream and downstream later.

## Current Landscape

### Why this market matters now

Water compliance complexity is rising, not falling.

- EPA states there are **more than 148,000 public water systems in the United States**, serving about 90% of Americans. That creates a large regulated base even before adding stormwater permittees, industrial facilities, developers, and engineering service providers. Source: [EPA public water systems overview](https://www.epa.gov/dwreginfo/information-about-public-water-systems).
- EPA's **Lead and Copper Rule Improvements** require drinking water systems to identify and replace lead pipes within 10 years and expand testing and communications obligations. Source: [EPA LCRI page](https://www.epa.gov/ground-water-and-drinking-water/lead-and-copper-rule-improvements).
- EPA's **CCR rule revisions**, announced on **May 15, 2024**, make annual water quality reports more prescriptive and, starting in **2027**, require systems serving over 10,000 customers to distribute reports twice per year. Source: [EPA CCR revisions](https://www.epa.gov/ccr/consumer-confidence-report-rule-revisions).
- EPA's PFAS regulatory posture remains active and somewhat fluid. As of **May 14, 2025**, EPA said it would keep MCLs for **PFOA and PFOS**, while proposing more time for compliance and reconsidering parts of the broader 2024 PFAS rule. Source: [EPA PFAS announcement](https://www.epa.gov/newsreleases/epa-announces-it-will-keep-maximum-contaminant-levels-pfoa-pfos).
- Federal capital continues to flow into the sector. EPA says the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law provides **$50 billion** for water, wastewater, and stormwater infrastructure upgrades. Source: [EPA BIL SRF funding status](https://www.epa.gov/water-infrastructure/bipartisan-infrastructure-law-srf-funding-status).

The result is a market where organizations must produce more evidence, more reporting, and more auditable documentation while facing budget and staffing pressure. AWWA's 2025 State of the Water Industry highlights that capital funding is the top challenge, and utilities are struggling to fund new technologies and upgrades. Source: [AWWA State of the Water Industry](https://www.awwa.org/state-of-the-water-industry/).

### Core concepts

#### NPDES

The National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System is the primary federal permitting framework for water discharges. In practice, it creates many recurring compliance documents at federal, state, and local levels.

#### SWPPP

A **Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plan** is a site-specific plan describing how a project or facility will prevent pollutant runoff. It is highly document-centric and commonly paired with permit registration documents, inspections, corrective actions, and certifications.

#### MS4

Municipal Separate Storm Sewer System permits require municipalities and similar entities to document inspections, illicit discharge activity, construction oversight, post-construction controls, public education, and annual reporting.

#### CCR

A **Consumer Confidence Report** is the annual drinking-water quality report community water systems must deliver to customers. It is a structured, recurring, public-facing compliance document with changing EPA requirements.

#### CROMERR and state filing systems

Electronic reporting increasingly matters. California's SMARTS system, for example, is explicitly described as CROMERR-compliant and serves as a digital hub for permit registration documents, compliance, and monitoring data. Source: [California SMARTS](https://www.waterboards.ca.gov/water_issues/programs/stormwater/smarts/).

## Recent Developments

### Regulatory developments

- **Lead and Copper Rule Improvements** finalized by EPA on **October 8, 2024**. This raises reporting, inventory, notification, and replacement obligations. Source: [EPA LCRI](https://www.epa.gov/ground-water-and-drinking-water/lead-and-copper-rule-improvements).
- **Consumer Confidence Report revisions** finalized on **May 15, 2024**, with key changes taking effect in **2027**. Source: [EPA CCR revisions](https://www.epa.gov/ccr/consumer-confidence-report-rule-revisions).
- **PFAS compliance** remains a moving target. EPA's current posture preserves the PFOA/PFOS standards but extends or proposes extending compliance timelines and reconsiders parts of the broader rule. Source: [EPA PFAS update](https://www.epa.gov/newsreleases/epa-announces-it-will-keep-maximum-contaminant-levels-pfoa-pfos).

### Market developments

- Water compliance software is consolidating. SwiftComply's 2025 acquisitions show that vendors want broader compliance suites spanning adjacent programs.
- GIS and field workflows are increasingly table stakes in stormwater software. OpenGov, NPDESPro, and 2NDNATURE all emphasize field data capture, inspection workflows, and ArcGIS integration.
- Buyers increasingly expect software to reduce manual report assembly, not just store records.

### Implication for Anvil

This is a good market for a document infrastructure wedge, but not for a generic platform pitch. Buyers already see vendors promising "complete compliance." Anvil needs a sharper message: **replace the most painful document assembly and signature loops first**.

## GTM Wedge Analysis

### Document-type options

#### Option 1: SWPPP and construction stormwater permit package

This includes the SWPPP itself plus permit registration documents such as NOI, project metadata, certifications, inspection forms, corrective-action notices, and closeout paperwork.

Why it is strong:

- High frequency in active construction markets.
- Time-sensitive; delays can stall site mobilization or expose firms to noncompliance.
- Template-heavy but not fully commoditized, which favors configurable workflow software.
- Multi-party data collection and signatures are common.
- Often prepared by private engineering firms or specialists with faster buying cycles than utilities.
- California explicitly requires SWPPP-related professional roles and digital workflows through SMARTS and the 2022 CGP ecosystem. Sources: [California CGP overview](https://www.waterboards.ca.gov/santaana/water_issues/programs/stormwater/construction_general_permit.html), [California 2022 CGP reissuance](https://www.waterboards.ca.gov/water_issues/programs/stormwater/construction/general_permit_reissuance.html).

Weakness:

- State and local variation is real, so template coverage must be deliberate.

#### Option 2: MS4 annual reports and supporting inspection/enforcement packets

Why it is attractive:

- Pain is proven. 2NFORM and NPDESPro both market heavily on automated annual reporting and reduced admin burden.
- Annual reports aggregate many upstream workflows, which opens expansion paths.

Weakness:

- Primary buyers are often municipalities or public agencies, which means slower procurement.
- Annual cadence can make it harder to show immediate recurring usage unless upstream inspections are in scope.

#### Option 3: Drinking-water Consumer Confidence Reports

Why it matters:

- Federally required, highly visible, and becoming more prescriptive in 2027.

Weakness:

- Annual or semiannual cadence is too low for the best initial wedge.
- Buyers are usually utilities, which slows sales.
- Much of the pain sits in data assembly and customer communications, where 120Water and utility-specific systems are already credible.

#### Option 4: Drinking-water lead/PFAS compliance packages

Why it matters:

- Regulatory urgency is high.

Weakness:

- The workflow often extends beyond documents into sampling, asset inventory, lab integration, and public communication. That pulls the solution toward a full operational platform, where Anvil is currently less differentiated.

### Recommended initial document type

**Recommendation:** start with the **stormwater construction compliance package**, centered on **SWPPP generation and revision workflows**, then expand to NOI, inspections, corrective actions, and closeout packets.

This is the highest-probability entry because it sits exactly where Anvil is strong: structured intake, PDF generation, multi-party signatures, document versioning, and orchestration across internal staff, contractors, and clients.

## Recommended Buyer Persona

### Primary buyer

**Stormwater / water resources practice leader or compliance operations lead at a regional engineering firm**

Why this buyer works:

- Owns delivery efficiency and margin.
- Feels staffing pressure directly.
- Can champion standardization across multiple PMs and junior engineers.
- Usually has enough authority to run a pilot without a city council or utility board process.

### Secondary buyer

**Principal environmental engineer or office lead at a specialty stormwater consultancy**

This buyer is strong when the firm sells compliance services directly and can convert time savings into gross-margin improvement.

### Economic buyer

**Managing principal, COO, or business-unit leader**

The close will often require a margin and capacity story, not just a compliance story.

## Recommended Firm Size

### Best initial segment: 20-200 employees

More specifically, target firms with:

- 5-40 professionals touching stormwater/compliance documents
- enough repeat project volume to create workflow pain
- no appetite to build custom internal software
- enough operational maturity to standardize templates and approvals

### Why not very small firms first

Firms under roughly 10 employees may love the product, but many will still tolerate manual workflows and produce too little annual volume to create efficient customer acquisition.

### Why not large firms first

Large national AEC firms have bigger budgets but longer security review, more stakeholders, and a stronger bias toward incumbent enterprise platforms.

## Recommended Geography

### 1. California

Best initial market because:

- strong stormwater compliance culture
- mature digital filing through SMARTS
- explicit QSD/QSP roles under the 2022 Construction General Permit
- high project and permitting complexity
- many engineering/service firms already organized around these workflows

California's state materials explicitly describe construction permit coverage for projects disturbing one acre or more and the use of SMARTS for permit registration and annual reports. Sources: [California construction permit page](https://www.waterboards.ca.gov/santaana/water_issues/programs/stormwater/construction_general_permit.html), [SMARTS resources](https://www.waterboards.ca.gov/water_issues/programs/stormwater/smarts/).

### 2. Texas

Texas is the best fast follow because construction stormwater permitting is broad and the market is large. TCEQ requires projects disturbing five or more acres, or part of a larger common plan, to meet defined permit steps before discharge. Source: [TCEQ construction stormwater](https://www.tceq.texas.gov/permitting/stormwater/construction/TXR15_5_plus_steps.html/).

### 3. Fast-follow states

Florida, North Carolina, Colorado, Arizona, and Washington are logical next markets because they combine growth, construction activity, stormwater scrutiny, or established MS4 compliance routines.

## Pricing Model

### Recommendation: platform fee plus usage bands

Use a **hybrid model**:

- annual platform fee for the business unit or practice
- included volume for active projects or completed compliance packets
- overage or higher tier pricing based on additional projects, submissions, or completed signature packets

### Why this fits the segment

- Pure seat pricing mismatches the economic value, because the pain is per project and per packet, not per employee login.
- Pure usage pricing can feel risky to firms trying to standardize across teams.
- Hybrid pricing maps to both the operational buyer and the economic buyer.

### Suggested packaging

- **Pilot / design partner:** fixed-fee pilot
- **Growth tier:** annual platform plus project-volume band
- **Enterprise tier:** annual minimum plus API / integration / premium support

Assumption: exact price points should be set from pilot evidence, but the commercial posture should mirror Anvil's existing strength in usage-linked economics rather than force a generic seat model.

## Pilot Structure

### Recommended pilot

**Duration:** 90 days  
**Scope:** 10-25 active projects in one state, ideally California  
**Workflows:** SWPPP intake, PDF generation, routing, signatures, revision control, and one downstream filing packet  
**Templates:** top 3-5 recurring document types only  
**Users:** one practice leader, two to five PMs/engineers, one compliance coordinator, one admin or operations stakeholder

### Pilot success metrics

- reduction in prep time per packet
- reduction in turnaround time from project kickoff to submittal-ready packet
- reduction in missing fields or resubmission loops
- percent of projects run through the standardized workflow
- qualitative user score from PMs and preparers

### Design principle

Do not promise a full compliance system. Promise a **document throughput win** in one workflow family, with metrics visible inside one quarter.

## ROI Story

### Primary ROI narrative

**Recover high-value engineer time and compress compliance-cycle time.**

This story is more compelling than general admin savings because engineering labor is expensive and often billable.

### Quantifiable ROI components

1. **Engineer and PM hours saved**
   - Assumption: if a firm saves even 2-6 hours per packet across dozens or hundreds of packets, the annual value is material.
2. **Faster project mobilization**
   - Construction compliance delays can slow site starts or create client friction.
3. **Lower rework**
   - Standardized intake and templating reduce missing fields, wrong versions, and signature churn.
4. **Higher delivery margin**
   - Work shifts from senior staff doing document assembly to lower-cost review or automated generation.
5. **Auditability and defensibility**
   - Centralized records reduce scramble during regulator inquiries or client disputes.

### Supporting market evidence

- NPDESPro features user testimony that the platform may have "cut out the need for an extra administrative position." Source: [NPDESPro](https://www.npdespro.com/).
- 2NFORM states annual reports can take "minutes, not months" once data is captured in the system. Source: [2NFORM for municipalities](https://www.2ndnaturewater.com/2nform-for-cities-and-municipalities/).

These are vendor claims, not neutral benchmarks, but they reinforce that buyers already understand the economic burden of manual compliance reporting.

## Practical Opportunities, Risks, and Open Questions

### Opportunities

### Practical opportunities

- **Consultant-first GTM**: faster buying cycles than municipalities.
- **California design partners**: strong wedge quality and credible logos.
- **Template library moat**: state- and workflow-specific document packs can compound into defensibility.
- **Embedded partner strategy**: stormwater consultants can bring Anvil into client environments.
- **Expansion path**: from SWPPP into MS4 construction oversight, industrial stormwater, and later selected drinking-water reporting.

### Strategic upside

If Anvil wins the document layer first, it can later decide whether to remain infrastructure or climb into vertical functionality. That is a healthier sequencing than trying to build a full water-compliance suite immediately.

### Risks

### Competitive risk

Vertical vendors already market compliance outcomes, not just documents. If Anvil sounds like a generic forms product, it will lose.

### Integration risk

Some customers will expect GIS, asset, lab, or state-portal integrations earlier than Anvil may want to build them.

### Variability risk

State-by-state and agency-by-agency differences can erode standardization if Anvil expands too quickly.

### Procurement and trust risk

Water and infrastructure buyers care about security, audit trails, and regulatory defensibility. Anvil has relevant security/compliance messaging, but it will need sector-specific proof points. Source: [Anvil security and compliance](https://www.useanvil.com/products/workflows/features/security-and-compliance/).

### Positioning risk

If Anvil chases both utilities and consultants at launch, it will dilute the message. The motion, ROI story, and product asks are different.

### Open Questions

1. Does Anvil want to win as a **horizontal infrastructure layer** or eventually become a **vertical application** in water compliance?
2. How much state-specific template and regulatory logic is Anvil willing to own directly?
3. Can Anvil support customer expectations around integrations with state portals, GIS systems, and document repositories?
4. Will the first customer be a consultant, a specialist stormwater service provider, or a municipality?
5. Can Anvil secure 3-5 California design partners willing to co-build the initial template library and reference story?

## What to Monitor in the Next 12 Months

- EPA follow-through on proposed PFAS compliance-date changes and any final rule adjustments.
- State implementation of the 2024 CCR revisions ahead of the 2027 effective changes.
- How quickly LCRI obligations turn into software budget and workflow redesign.
- Additional consolidation among water compliance vendors, especially in stormwater and utility reporting.
- Whether states or major municipalities tighten digital-submission and audit-trail expectations.
- Budget pressure in utilities and local governments, which may increase consultant outsourcing and favor consultant-first GTM.
- Whether competitors begin marketing document-generation AI specifically for engineering compliance packets.

## Actionable Next Steps

1. **Choose California construction stormwater as the initial beachhead.** Narrow the ICP to regional engineering firms and specialist stormwater providers, not utilities.
2. **Package the wedge around one named workflow family:** SWPPP generation, revisions, NOI packet assembly, inspections, corrective actions, and closeout.
3. **Recruit 3-5 California design partners** with repeated project volume and a clear QSD/QSP-led workflow.
4. **Build a state-specific template pack** for California first, including intake schema, packet logic, signature routing, and document checklisting.
5. **Run fixed-fee 90-day pilots** across 10-25 active projects with explicit success metrics tied to prep time, resubmissions, and turnaround.
6. **Sell the ROI in recovered engineering capacity, not generic admin automation.** Make the business case in hours, margin, and schedule protection.
7. **Price as platform plus usage bands** tied to project or packet volume, with implementation priced separately during the design-partner phase.
8. **Delay utility-direct expansion** until Anvil has reference customers, a stronger template moat, and clearer answers on integrations and procurement requirements.
9. **Prepare a fast-follow Texas package** once California pilots validate the operating model and template economics.
10. **Define a crisp competitive position:** Anvil is the fastest way to standardize, generate, route, and audit compliance documents, not a replacement for every GIS, asset, or compliance database.
